3.1. Children's Literature

Definition and Origins

Children's literature consists of the body of written works and accompanying illustrations produced to entertain or instruct young people. The genre encompasses a wide range of works, including acknowledged classics of world literature, picture books and easy-to-read stories written exclusively for children, and fairy tales, lullabies, fables, folk songs, and other primarily orally transmitted materials.

It first clearly emerged as a distinct and independent form of literature in the second half of the 18th century, before which it had been at best only in an embryonic stage. During the 20th century, however, its growth has been luxuriant, establishing its place as a fully recognized branch of literature.

Genres of Children's Literature

Categorizing books is not a science; there are grey areas and subtypes. Here are the core genres:

  • Contemporary realistic fiction: Stories that could happen to people or animals in the present. Characters are made up, but their actions and feelings are highly realistic (family/animal/sports stories, adventures, and mysteries).
  • Historical fiction: Set in a historical period, blending authentic historical settings/facts with imaginary characters and plots (written about a time in which the author has not lived).
  • Non-fiction or information books: Emphasize documented facts. They inform and entertain by excellence of presentation, illustration, and research.
  • Biography and Autobiography: Story of an actual person's life notable for accomplishments or significant triumphs. Autobiography is written by the person themselves.
  • Traditional literature: Ancient stories or poems originating in oral storytelling with no known original authors (myths, epics, legends, tall tales, fables, and religious stories).
  • Poetry: Artistic expression of ideas and feelings through rhythmical compositions of imaginative and beautiful carefully selected words.
  • Modern fantasy: Events, settings, or characters lie completely outside the realm of possibility. The author must create an internally logical and consistent world to suspended disbelief (fairytale by known authors, animal fantasy, toy personification, quest stories, time travel).
  • Science fiction (Futuristic fiction): Imaginative literature where unusual events occur under logical scientific principles or technical possibilities.
  • Picture books (Format, NOT a genre): Profusely illustrated books in which pictures are absolutely essential to complete the understanding and enjoyment of the story.

Historical Evolution in EFL Classes

  • 19th Century: Under the Grammar Translation Method (GTM), literature predominated as students spent major class times translating literary texts into native tongues.
  • 1960s - Late 1970s: Ignored. Under the Structuralist Approach, language learning was seen as a matter of linguistics focused on correctness of grammatical form and habit formation rather than content or style. Direct Method, Audiolingualism, TPR, and the Silent Way also ignored literature.
  • Late 1970s - Early 1980s: Ignored. The Communicative Approach initially favored "usable, practical" oral dialogues, leaving no space for literature in the curriculum.
  • Since 1980s (The Return): Highly valued to improve communicative competence, critical thinking, aesthetic appreciation, and cultural awareness.

Benefits of Using Literature (Collie & Slater 1991)

Offers four key benefits: a) Authentic material (exposing learners to fresh, native-level structures), b) Cultural enrichment (seeing the world through others' eyes), c) Language advancement (increasing competence across all skills), and d) Personal growth (raising intellectual and emotional development).

3.2. Short Stories: Selection Criteria and Use in ELT

Why Use Short Stories? Overcoming Objections

Public schools often raise objections against literature due to overcrowded classes, overloaded syllabuses, and limited time. However, short stories successfully resolve these issues compared to other genres:

  • Poetry: Deviated and figurative language takes too long to grasp.
  • Novels: Length makes them difficult to finish within limited class hours.
  • Drama: Difficult to act out inside crowded public classrooms.
  • Short Stories: Edgar Allan Poe defines a short story as a narrative read in one sitting (from 30 minutes to 2 hours), aimed at a 'single unique effect' with one plot, few characters, and simple settings. Easy for learners to follow.

Collie & Slater (1991) Advantages of Short Stories

  1. Practicality: Length allows them to be covered entirely in one or two class sessions.
  2. Simplicity: Not too complicated for students to work with on their own.
  3. Variety of choice: High variety to match different interests and tastes.
  4. Utility: Applicable across all levels, age groups, and class hours.

Criteria for Choosing the Text

Story selection is a key teacher role. To make learning student-centered, consider these criteria:

  • Hill (1994) Basic Criteria: 1) Needs and abilities of students, 2) linguistic and stylistic level of the text, 3) amount of background information required to appreciate the material.
  • Ur (1996) Readability: Using unsimplified 'authentic' texts with less proficient learners is frustrating and counterproductive; simplified or graded texts are highly recommended.
  • Spack (1985) & McKay (2001) Engagement: Choose stories relevant to life experience, matching teacher interest, or which have film adaptations to provide visual interpretations.

Language Skills Development

  • Reading: Lao and Krashen (2000) Hong Kong university study revealed that students reading literary texts made much better improvement in vocabulary and reading comprehension than those reading non-literary texts.
  • Writing: Serves as both a model and subject matter. Oster (1989) affirms it helps students write more creatively.
  • Speaking and Listening: Developed via oral reading, dramatizations, roleplays, re-enactments, and classroom discussions, which also directly improve pronunciation.

3.3. Storybooks and Storytelling in ELT

The Rise of Storytelling in Primary Education

Primary English teachers now use acquisition-based methodologies to create acquisition-rich environments. Handbooks solely devoted to this technique include Wright (1995, 1997), Zaro & Salaberri (1995), Gerngross & Puchta (1996), and Mourão (2003).

Additionally, the globalization of English has brought 'world Englishes' to classrooms, where storybooks serve to expose children to rich international accents and varieties. Since each child is unique, storybooks cater to different intelligences and learner profiles.

Key Reasons for Using Storybooks

  • Motivating & Enjoyable: Stories build highly positive attitudes toward foreign languages and cultures.
  • Exercise Imagination: Personally involve students as they identify with characters and illustrations.
  • Fantasy to Reality Link: Enable children to forge links between home and school.
  • Shared Social Experience: Provokes shared responses of laughter, excitement, and anticipation, building class cohesion.
  • Repetition & Memorability: Natural repetitive context of key vocabulary allows items to enter speech effortlessly.
  • Acquisition-based Input: Applies Krashen's input hypothesis by offering optimal language input slightly above the level they are expected to produce.
  • Develop Learning Strategies: Fosters predicting, guessing meaning, and active listening using:
    • a. Visual clues (high-quality pictures and illustrations)
    • b. Audio clues (sound effects, onomatopoeia)
    • c. Prior language and prior world knowledge

The Whole-School Educational Panning

  • Cross-curricular links: Link English with Math (counting, time, measuring), Science (insect cycles, animal life), Computing (using tech safely and purposefully), Art (making cards, collages, puppets), Geography (map/atlas use), History (chronology, prehistory), and PE (moving like animals).
  • Learning to Learn: Fosters autonomous learning through memory training, predicting, hypothesizing, and using/creating dictionaries.
  • Conceptual reinforcement: Reinforces concepts of color, size, shape, time, and cause-effect (e.g., using 'Princess Smartypants' to learn cause-effect).
  • Citizenship, diversity and multicultural education: Raises intercultural awareness and raises tolerance. Explicit focus stories like 'Something Else' promote acceptance, while cultural stories like 'The Kangaroo from Woolloomooloo' or 'The Clever Tortoise' illustrate cultural differences alongside shared global trickster themes.
